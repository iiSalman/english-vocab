Suits — general vocabulary

76 words across all seasons and episodes.

injunction

n C1

an official court order that forces someone to do something or, more commonly, to stop doing something.

S1E4 — "If you don't sign my injunction, there's going to be a lawsuit."

drives the central legal conflict of multiple episodes — Harvey wins or loses cases on whether he can get one issued or overturned.

escrow

n C2

money or assets held by a neutral third party until the conditions of an agreement are met.

S1E1 — "I received confirmation of a wire transfer from escrow indicating payment in full."

Harvey uses it as a power move in the opening scene to prove the deal is already locked in.

deposition

n C1

a formal sworn statement made out of court, used as evidence at trial.

S1E6 — "We'll see how confident you are after my deposition."

depositions are where most of the season's legal pressure is applied — a frequent battleground for the lawyers.

subpoena

n C1

an official legal document ordering a person to appear in court or produce documents.

S1E5 — "I'll have a subpoena on his desk by morning."

a recurring weapon throughout the season — characters constantly threaten or issue them.

perjury

n C1

the criminal offence of telling a lie in court after swearing to tell the truth.

S1E7 — "If he testifies to that, it's perjury."

a key risk for several characters in S1 — the threat of perjury changes who is willing to testify.

retainer

n C1

a fee paid in advance to a lawyer to secure their services.

S1E6 — "Before we get started, there's something in the retainer that Tom pointed out."

signals the formal start of a lawyer-client relationship — comes up whenever a new client is signed.

plaintiff

n C1

the person who brings a case against another in a court of law.

S1E4 — "Our client is the plaintiff here, not the defendant."

appears constantly — knowing which side is the plaintiff and which is the defendant is essential to following any scene in court.

litigation

n C1

the process of taking legal action; a lawsuit.

S1E4 — "I've decided to leave the bench. Try my hand at litigation."

the firm's whole business — the word appears whenever the season's larger strategy is discussed.

disbarred

adj/v C2

officially banned from practising as a lawyer.

S1E11 — "In which case, I'll have you disbarred."

the ultimate professional threat — used at the season's most charged moments. Carries far more weight than "fired".

overturn

v C1

to officially decide that an earlier legal decision was wrong and change it.

S1E4 — "When the next judge sees all the facts, he's going to overturn your ruling."

the standard verb for undoing rulings on appeal — appears whenever a court loss is being challenged.

devious

adj C1

willing to lie and trick people to get what you want.

S1E8 — "Kyle is cocky and devious."

the show keeps describing characters with this word — it's central to how the firm sizes up rivals and even their own people.

vindictive

adj C1

deliberately trying to harm someone in revenge for something they did to you.

S1E12 — "I'm not being vindictive."

drives multiple characters' motivations — especially when grudges from earlier episodes resurface.

patronizing

adj C1

speaking to or treating someone as if you are more important or intelligent than them.

S1E1 — "It's just so patronizing when you say that he can handle those things."

Louis's defining complaint — the word captures the office hierarchy and his resentment of Harvey.

condescending

adj C1

behaving as though you are better or more important than other people; very close to *patronizing*.

S1E1 — Louis complains about Harvey's condescending tone.

pairs with *patronizing* throughout the season; both describe the social tension you'll hear in almost every Louis/Harvey scene.

sterling

adj C1

excellent in quality, especially when describing a reputation or character.

S1E4 — "But that sterling reputation that you value so highly? Down the tubes."

used twice in S1 to discuss a lawyer's professional reputation — a common collocation: *sterling reputation*.

smoking gun

idiom C1

a piece of evidence that proves clearly and finally that someone is guilty.

S1E5 — "Where's my smoking gun? The background check that confirmed Stan lied about his credentials."

recurs throughout the season — Harvey is constantly looking for one. Knowing the idiom is essential to following case strategy talk.

come clean

idiom C1

to admit the full truth about something you have been hiding.

S1E3 — "Quentin wants to come clean."

appears at the climax of multiple episodes — a character "coming clean" usually shifts the entire case.

blow the whistle

idiom C1

to publicly report wrongdoing, usually in an organisation you belong to.

S1E8 — "You can blow the whistle on me if you want to."

plot-critical idiom in the corporate-fraud storyline — used as both a threat and an offer of redemption.

out of line

idiom C1

behaving in a way that is unacceptable, rude, or beyond what is allowed.

S1E1 — "Louis, I apologize. I was out of line."

the standard apology phrase in workplace conflicts — you'll hear it whenever an argument cools down.

quid pro quo

n C2

a Latin phrase meaning a favour or advantage given in exchange for something of equal value.

S1E4 — "Perhaps we could discuss a little quid pro quo before I do."

a signature legal-thriller phrase — the negotiating tool Harvey and his rivals reach for when they want a clean trade.

the ball is in your court

idiom C1

it is now your turn to act or make a decision.

S1E1 — "So, I'd say the ball's in your court."

Harvey's go-to closing line in negotiations — repeated by other characters when they want to pressure someone for a response.

proclivities

n C2

tendencies to behave or think in a particular way, especially one that is criticised.

S1E2 — "If someone of his generation who shares the same proclivities…"

Louis uses formal vocabulary to soften ugly suggestions — recognising this kind of word is key to catching when a character is hiding intent behind politeness.

barge in

phrasal verb C1

to enter a room or interrupt a conversation rudely and without permission.

S1E2 — "You don't barge in here when I'm on the phone."

the boundary-setting verb of the office scenes — characters constantly accuse each other of barging in.

woo

v C1

to try to persuade someone to support you, especially as a customer or romantic partner.

S1E2 — "Back in the day, to woo a client, you would take them to dinner."

in *Suits* it always describes the business of attracting clients — the verb behind every client-pitch scene.

cachet

n C2

the quality of being respected or admired, especially because of being associated with something prestigious.

S1E1 — "The fact that we only hire from Harvard gives us a cachet."

explains the firm's whole hiring philosophy in one word — and a useful term whenever the show contrasts prestige with merit.

hedge fund

n phrase C1

a pooled investment fund that uses aggressive strategies to seek high returns for wealthy clients.

S1E3 — "It never hurts to have a man who runs a two billion dollar hedge fund owe you one."

introduces the wealthy-client world Harvey operates in — the term recurs whenever a major finance client is on screen.

ratify

v C1

to give formal approval or consent to a decision, treaty, or contract.

S1E3 — "Tomorrow, the Board will ratify the sale of the factory."

the verb that makes a corporate decision official — central to the deal-closing tension of the episode.

back off

phrasal verb C1

to retreat from a confrontation or stop pursuing something.

S1E3 — "Back off Stensland, and close the deal."

the standard demand in office power-plays — Harvey uses it as both threat and order.

plausible deniability

n phrase C2

the ability to credibly deny knowledge of or responsibility for an action — used to protect a leader from blame.

S1E3 — "Either Stensland's out and we double our billings, or you have plausible deniability and he stays with the firm."

a signature corporate/political concept — describes a strategic stance the firm's senior partners take to avoid liability.

on the line

idiom C1

at risk of being lost; in jeopardy.

S1E3 — "My ass is on the line."

the stakes-raising phrase used whenever a character's reputation, job, or case could collapse.

testify

v C1

to give evidence as a witness, especially in a court of law.

S1E4 — "I tracked down 15 former tenants who are willing to testify to all manner of harassment."

every legal storyline turns on who is willing to testify — central courtroom verb across the season.

mitigate

v C1

to make a problem, harm, or punishment less severe.

S1E4 — "If you don't tell us the real facts, we can't mitigate the damage from his lie."

lawyers' word for damage-control — used whenever the firm is doing repair work on a bad situation.

liable

adj C1

legally responsible for something, especially for paying damages.

S1E4 — "The company may be liable for actual damages, but we can shift the blame for the fraud to Lisa."

the core question of nearly every civil case the firm handles — who is liable for what.

extortion

n C1

the criminal act of obtaining money or favours through threats or pressure.

S1E4 — "I don't take to extortion."

Harvey throws this word back at opponents who try to strong-arm him — a recurring power-move accusation.

pending

prep C1

while waiting for; until something happens. Also adjective: about to happen.

S1E4 — "Pending the outcome of a rent abatement hearing? That's not in the code."

a useful formal preposition for legal/business writing — describes the limbo state between filing and ruling.

take the Fifth

idiom C2

in US law, to refuse to answer a question by invoking the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination.

S1E5 — "What color was the light? I take the Fifth."

an iconic American legal reference — Mike uses it as a joke, but the show returns to the real concept in later seasons.

warrant

n C1

an official legal document authorising a search, arrest, or other action.

S1E5 — "No one sets foot in there without a warrant, a Papal edict, or my say-so."

without one, evidence is often inadmissible — comes up whenever law-enforcement or discovery is discussed.

negligence

n C1

failure to take the care that the law requires, leading to harm or loss.

S1E5 — "Employers are responsible for their employees' negligence."

the foundation of most civil-injury cases — describes the legal threshold for being sued for an accident.

liability

n C1

the state of being legally responsible for something; a debt or obligation that one is responsible for.

S1E5 — "Vicarious liability applies to discrimination, harassment, and accidents."

the noun form of being *liable* — the technical term lawyers use for legal exposure to losses.

class action

n phrase C1

a lawsuit filed by one or more people on behalf of a larger group with the same claim.

S1E5 — "Louis won the class action suit?"

a recurring case type for the firm — important to understand the difference from individual lawsuits.

ambiguous

adj C1

open to more than one interpretation; unclear or vague.

S1E5 — "Ambiguous."

what a lawyer flags whenever contract language could be read two ways — a key technical objection.

yield

v C1

to give way to other traffic; to surrender or concede.

S1E5 — "Thinking you don't have to yield? You ran a red light."

the driving sense in this scene, but the broader meaning — to concede ground in negotiation — recurs throughout the show.

above board

idiom C1

open, honest, and legal; with nothing hidden.

S1E6 — "As your lawyer, I need to be able to tell the DOJ that we're all above board."

the legal/ethical bar a client must clear — Harvey uses it to draw the line between aggressive and criminal.

inclined

adj C1

tending toward a particular opinion or action; somewhat willing.

S1E6 — "The DOJ might be inclined to start rattling cages again."

a softer, formal way to predict someone's behaviour — common in legal and diplomatic language.

insider trading

n phrase C2

the illegal practice of trading securities based on non-public information.

S1E6 — "Did you help Gabby when she was charged with insider trading?"

the specific white-collar crime that drives much of the season's plot — the term you must recognise instantly.

proprietary

adj C1

belonging to or controlled by a specific company, often legally protected from others' use.

S1E6 — "Our current positions, that's proprietary information."

describes the boundary between what a firm can share and what it must protect — appears wherever trade secrets are at stake.

merger

n C1

the combining of two companies or organisations into one.

S1E7 — "Remind me to be irritated with you for not billing more on the Swindon merger."

the firm's bread-and-butter transaction type — dozens of plotlines revolve around brokering one.

hostile takeover

n phrase C2

the acquisition of one company by another against the wishes of the target's management.

S1E7 — "It's a hostile takeover."

the specific dramatic flavour of merger that drives confrontations — distinct from a friendly acquisition.

preliminary

adj C1

coming before something more important; introductory.

S1E7 — "Do you have the preliminary proposal?"

the standard adjective for the early-stage documents and hearings that set up later legal moves.

plead

v C1

to make an emotional appeal; in law, to formally state your position (e.g. plead guilty).

S1E7 — "Looks like Ross here came to plead for mercy."

both the everyday "beg" meaning and the legal "enter a plea" meaning recur — knowing the dual sense helps disambiguate scenes.

hearsay

n C1

information you heard from someone else but cannot verify; in law, evidence that is generally inadmissible.

S1E7 — "Hearsay."

the most common one-word objection lawyers shout — instantly signals that the other side's evidence won't hold up.

scapegoat

n C1

a person blamed for the mistakes or wrongdoing of others.

S1E8 — "Well, he's the perfect scapegoat."

the entire episode's structure — the firm is choosing who takes the blame.

make amends

idiom C1

to do something to correct a wrong or make up for past harm.

S1E8 — "A company that's trying to make amends for what it did."

the phrase used whenever a character is offered a chance at redemption — a recurring moral pivot.

incentive

n C1

something that motivates or encourages someone to do something.

S1E8 — "It was an incentive."

common business and legal term — used by both sides to label a payment as either legitimate motivation or thinly veiled bribery.

hush money

n phrase C1

money paid to keep someone silent about something embarrassing or illegal.

S1E8 — "You either took your cut from Mazlo or it was hush money."

the loaded accusation that reframes a payment as a cover-up — critical to following the episode's central twist.

aggravate

v C1

to make a bad situation, illness, or feeling worse; also informally, to annoy.

S1E8 — "I came here to pay my respects, not aggravate a grieving woman."

more formal than "make worse" — common in legal English when describing damages or wrongdoing.

have a case

idiom C1

to have a strong enough legal argument to bring or continue a lawsuit.

S1E8 — "Without Perkins, you don't have a case."

the strategic test lawyers apply before going to court — used as a shorthand for whether the lawsuit is viable.

breach

n C1

the act of breaking a law, agreement, or expected standard; also a gap in defences or security.

S1E9 — "We've discovered a breach at the firm."

a versatile legal noun — *breach of contract*, *breach of trust*, *security breach* — all variations the show uses.

collateral

n C1

something pledged as security for a loan, to be taken if the loan is not repaid.

S1E9 — "They won't give us anything without collateral."

common in financial dialogue throughout the season — without it, deals stall.

get away with

phrasal verb C1

to do something wrong or risky without being caught or punished.

S1E9 — "You're not going to get away with what you're doing."

the threat-line of nearly every confrontation in the show — describes the central tension of cheating versus consequences.

betray

v C1

to be disloyal to someone who trusts you; to reveal secrets or act against an ally.

S1E9 — "Why would Louis betray the firm?"

the loyalty/betrayal axis is central to S1's arc — every key relationship is tested against this word.

cover one's tracks

idiom C1

to hide evidence of one's actions so they cannot be discovered.

S1E9 — "He may break the rules, but he covers his tracks."

the descriptor for a careful wrongdoer — used both to admire and to indict.

waiver

n C1

the giving up of a legal right, often in writing.

S1E10 — "They signed a conflict of interest waiver."

what lawyers ask clients to sign whenever a potential issue must be acknowledged in advance — common procedural step.

precedent

n C1

an earlier event or legal decision that serves as a guide for future cases.

S1E10 — "I want you to find legal precedent that will allow us to subpoena those records."

the backbone of common-law legal argument — Mike's research is almost always a hunt for precedent.

irrevocable

adj C2

unable to be changed, reversed, or undone.

S1E10 — "Such appointment is irrevocable and shall be deemed a power coupled with…"

boilerplate contract language that signals a permanent commitment — recognising it stops you skimming over key clauses.

conflict of interest

n phrase C1

a situation where someone's personal interests could compromise their professional duties.

S1E10 — "They signed a conflict of interest waiver."

the ethical line every lawyer must check before taking a case — central to the firm's risk management.

prosecutor

n C1

a lawyer who conducts the case against a defendant in a criminal trial.

S1E11 — "Did you know that Harvey was a prosecutor?"

explains Harvey's background and aggression — the noun that frames his negotiating style.

take the fall

idiom C1

to accept the blame or punishment for something, often to protect someone else.

S1E11 — "I'm not going to let him take the fall for that asshole."

the moral choice multiple characters face in S1 — whether to absorb the consequences for an ally.

in the clear

idiom C1

free from suspicion, blame, or danger.

S1E11 — "But you're in the clear, Harvey."

the relief-line at the end of a long fight — paired with *on the line* (S1E3) as the show's risk-status idioms.

disclose

v C1

to make secret or new information known.

S1E11 — "You sure there isn't anything that you want to disclose?"

the precise legal verb behind ethical obligations — what lawyers must do, and what clients often try to avoid.

intimidate

v C1

to frighten or pressure someone, especially to influence their behaviour.

S1E11 — "Do you really think, by deposing him on the stand, you're going to intimidate him?"

the tactic, and the accusation — characters in the show alternately use intimidation and accuse each other of it.

cut a deal

idiom C1

to reach an agreement, especially one involving compromise or trade-offs.

S1E12 — "I thought it best to cut a deal."

the entire premise of the finale revolves around whether to fight on or cut a deal — the show's central strategic verb.

prosecute

v C1

to formally bring a legal case against someone in court.

S1E12 — "My job is to prosecute."

the verb form of *prosecutor* (S1E11) — together they cover both the role and the act.

leverage

n C1

an advantage that can be used to influence others; power gained from a strategic position.

S1E12 — "This is my leverage."

Harvey's most-used noun — every confrontation reduces to who has it and how to keep it.

compromise

v C1

to lower one's standards or principles, especially in a damaging way; also to settle a dispute by mutual concession.

S1E12 — "You've compromised your principles before."

carries two distinct senses in the show — the positive *settle a dispute* and the negative *damage your integrity* — context matters.

sentence

n C1

the punishment given by a court to someone found guilty of a crime.

S1E12 — "The State advises that we will be seeking the maximum sentence of life in prison."

the stakes-defining noun in any criminal case — the difference between *case dismissed* and *life in prison*.
