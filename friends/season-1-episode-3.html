cushion the blow

idiom C1

to soften the impact of bad news or a painful experience.

S1E3 — Joey explaining dating euphemisms: "Cushions the blow. Like your parents putting the dog to sleep and saying it went to a farm."

elegant idiom for breaking bad news gently — common in HR, medicine, journalism, relationships.

minion

n C1

a low-ranking follower or servant of a powerful person — often used humorously or with disdain.

S1E3 — Phoebe about the bank crediting her too much money: "Satan's minions at work again!"

very common in fantasy, politics, and office sarcasm (*the boss's minions*). Took on extra cultural weight from the Despicable Me films.

karmic

adj C2

relating to karma; reflecting the moral consequences of past actions.

S1E3 — Phoebe about spending money that isn't hers: "It'd be this giant karmic debt."

signals an introspective, slightly spiritual register — common in modern English to talk about cause-and-effect outside religion.

pick off

phrasal verb C1

to remove or eliminate one at a time, often by targeting the weakest.

S1E3 — Rachel about her friends judging her dates: "Coyotes picking off the weak members of the herd."

evocative phrasal — used literally (snipers, predators) and figuratively (competitors, job candidates).

odds

n C1

the probability or likelihood that something will happen; the ratio of chances for vs. against.

S1E3 — Monica to Rachel: "The odds of that happening are slimmer if they never get to meet the guy."

essential vocabulary for probability, gambling, and forecasting — *the odds are good/against you*, *beat the odds*.

whimper

v C1

to make low, weak crying or complaining sounds — like a hurt animal.

S1E3 — Ross to Monica about losing the dog: "Can't I whimper a little bit?"

more specific than *cry* — implies quiet, pathetic distress. Also famous from T.S. Eliot: *"Not with a bang but a whimper."*

crooked

adj C1

bent or twisted out of shape; also figuratively dishonest or corrupt.

S1E3 — Phoebe praising Alan: "His smile was crooked. Like the man in the shoe."

two distinct uses — physical (*a crooked nose*) and moral (*a crooked politician*). Both very common.

yardstick

n C2

a standard or measure used to judge or compare; figuratively, a benchmark.

S1E3 — Rachel about Alan: "He is the yardstick by which future boyfriends will be measured."

formal/literary metaphor — common in essays, reviews, and management talk. Pairs with *benchmark*, *gold standard*.

innate

adj C2

present from birth; existing as a natural, inherent quality.

S1E3 — Chandler being absurdly sincere about Alan: "It's his innate 'Alan-ness' that we adore."

academic and psychological register — *innate talent*, *innate ability*, *innate goodness*. Opposite of *learned* or *acquired*.

flaw

n C1

an imperfection or weakness — in an object, plan, character, or argument.

S1E3 — Chandler defending his smoking: "So I have a flaw! Big deal!"

core vocabulary for criticism — *a fatal flaw*, *a character flaw*, *a flaw in the design*.

snort

v C1

to make a sudden, harsh sound through the nose, often when laughing or in disgust.

S1E3 — Chandler listing his friends' annoying habits: "Monica, who snorts when she laughs."

very specific sound-verb — also used for animals (*the horse snorted*) and as a noun (*a snort of laughter*).

endearing

adj C2

inspiring affection; charming in a sweet, sometimes clumsy way.

S1E3 — Phoebe about Joey's knuckle-cracking: "I think it's endearing."

the precise word for "annoying-but-cute" — often used to describe quirks, accents, or small habits in loved ones.

bottom line

idiom C1

the most important fact or final conclusion; figuratively the core point of an argument.

S1E3 — Chandler defiantly: "The bottom line is, smoking is cool, and you know it."

essential business and conversation phrase — *what's the bottom line?* cuts straight to the point.

déjà vu

n C1

the strange feeling that you have already experienced the current moment.

S1E3 — Phoebe: "Wait! I'm getting a déjà vu. No, I'm not."

borrowed from French and fully naturalized in English — used both literally (the eerie feeling) and figuratively (*it felt like déjà vu*).

let one's guard down

idiom C1

to relax one's emotional defences; to become vulnerable after being cautious.

S1E3 — Alan to Monica after the breakup: "You let your guard down. You start to care about someone, and…"

boxing metaphor for emotional risk — central to relationship and therapy talk.

can't stand

idiom C1

to strongly dislike; to be unable to tolerate someone or something.

S1E3 — Alan after breaking up: "I had a great time with you. I just can't stand your friends."

the most common informal way to express strong dislike in English — stronger than *don't like*, softer than *hate*.

gloves come off

idiom C2

the point at which people stop being polite and start fighting seriously — verbal or otherwise.

S1E3 — Chandler narrating after Monica snaps back: "The hair comes out and the gloves come off."

boxing-derived idiom signaling escalation — common in politics, debates, sports, and arguments. Often paired with *take the gloves off*.
