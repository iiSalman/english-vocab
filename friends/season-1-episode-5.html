maneuver

n C1

a skilful or clever movement or action, often requiring planning.

S1E5 — Chandler marvelling at bra removal: "You reach in, there's one maneuver and bam!"

used physically (driving, sports) and figuratively (politics, business — *a clever maneuver*). British spelling: *manoeuvre*.

rot in hell

idiom C1

a bitter curse wishing lasting suffering on someone you despise.

S1E5 — Phoebe about an ex: "Except for Kevin Millmore, may he rot in hell."

strong, dramatic expression of contempt — common in film, argument, and dark humour. Knowing its weight stops you using it casually.

gang up on

phrasal verb C1

to join with others to attack, criticise, or pressure one person.

S1E5 — Chandler about breaking up with Janice together: "She'd feel like we're ganging up on her."

core social-dynamics vocabulary — *don't gang up on me*, *they ganged up on the new kid*. Implies unfair numbers.

burn someone up

idiom C1

(informal) to make someone very angry or annoyed.

S1E5 — Rachel about her father: "That guy, he burns me up."

vivid informal anger idiom — distinct from literal burning. Pairs with *that really burns me up*.

eager

adj C1

keen and impatient to do or have something; full of enthusiasm.

S1E5 — Joey before approaching Angela: "I don't wanna seem too eager."

key word for describing motivation — *eager to please*, *eager to learn*. Pairs with the noun phrase *an eager beaver*.

accent

v C1

to emphasise or draw attention to a feature; to make something stand out.

S1E5 — Angela: "I'm wearing a dress that accents my boobs."

the verb sense (≠ the noun "accent" = pronunciation) is common in fashion, design, and cooking — *accent the waist*, *accented with gold*.

glamour

n C1

an attractive, exciting quality of luxury, beauty, or sophistication.

S1E5 — Rachel sarcastically: "Big glamour night. Me and Monica at Launderama."

evocative noun with adjective *glamorous* — used for celebrities, fashion, travel. Note the British -our spelling kept even in American English.

freakish

adj C1

very strange, abnormal, or unusual to the point of being startling.

S1E5 — Joey: "You wanna hear a freakish coincidence?"

stronger than *strange* — *a freakish accident*, *freakishly tall*. Related to *freak* (already known) but more formal in tone.

sophisticated

adj C1

worldly, refined, and culturally aware; also (of systems) complex and advanced.

S1E5 — Angela describing Bob: "He's smart, sophisticated, and he has a real job."

dual use — describing people (*a sophisticated palate*) and technology (*a sophisticated algorithm*). Essential review/writing vocabulary.

belch

v C1

to noisily release air from the stomach through the mouth; to burp.

S1E5 — Monica about a bad date: "your cousin who could belch the alphabet."

more formal/literary than *burp* — also figurative (*the factory belched smoke*). Knowing both registers is useful.

one of a kind

idiom C1

unique; the only one of its type, with no equal.

S1E5 — Joey about Angela: "She's like one of a kind, like a snowflake."

common compliment and marketing phrase — *a one-of-a-kind opportunity*, *truly one of a kind*. Hyphenated when used before a noun.

fuzzy

adj C1

covered in soft short hairs or fibres; also blurred, unclear, or imprecise.

S1E5 — Joey defending his fabric softener: "like a little fuzzy bear."

two senses — physical (*a fuzzy sweater*) and abstract (*fuzzy logic*, *the details are fuzzy*). Both very common.

supremely

adv C2

to the highest possible degree; extremely.

S1E5 — Janice: "I had the most supremely awful day."

strong intensifier with a slightly formal/dramatic flavour — *supremely confident*, *supremely talented*. Lifts your register above *very*.

blow off

phrasal verb C1

(informal) to deliberately ignore or skip something, or to treat someone dismissively.

S1E5 — Janice: "I blew off the afternoon. I went shopping."

very common American phrasal — *blow off a meeting*, *blow off steam* (different sense), *he blew me off*. Signals casual disregard.

brutal

adj C1

savagely violent; also used informally for anything harsh, painful, or unpleasant.

S1E5 — Joey about Chandler's hug with Janice: "the hug looked pretty brutal."

hugely versatile in modern English — *a brutal workout*, *brutal honesty*, *brutally cold*. The informal "very tough" sense is everywhere.

judgment call

idiom C1

a decision based on personal opinion rather than fixed rules, because no clear answer exists.

S1E5 — Ross on which load white cotton panties belong in: "That would be a judgment call."

essential workplace and everyday phrase — *I had to make a judgment call*. Signals discretion under uncertainty.

rambunctious

adj C2

noisy, energetic, and difficult to control; boisterous in a playful way.

S1E5 — Joey to Monica: "Like you've never got a little rambunctious with Ross."

colourful, slightly literary adjective for wild energy — *rambunctious kids*, *a rambunctious crowd*. Marks an advanced vocabulary.

grieve

v C1

to feel or express deep sorrow, especially over a loss or death.

S1E5 — Joey explaining Chandler's absence: "He needed time to grieve."

the precise verb for processing loss — *grieve for/over someone*. Related noun *grief* and adjective *grief-stricken*. Used literally and humorously.
