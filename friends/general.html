metaphor

n C1

a way of describing something by saying it is something else, to make a comparison.

S1E1 — Rachel: "What if I want to be a purse? Or a hat? No, I don't want you to buy me a hat! It's a metaphor, Daddy!"

the joke of the scene depends on knowing this word — her father takes the comparison literally.

matrimony

n C2

the state of being married; formal word for marriage, common in ceremonies.

S1E1 — "We are gathered here to join Joanie Louise Cunningham and Charles in the bonds of holy matrimony."

the kind of elevated vocabulary you hear at weddings — recognising it helps you follow formal English in films and ceremonies.

revelation

n C1

the act of making something previously secret known; a surprising piece of information.

S1E1 — "It's more of a fifth-date kind of revelation."

Paul uses it to soften an awkward confession — the word's slightly formal weight makes the moment funnier.

shred

v C1

to tear or cut something into very small pieces.

S1E1 — "The worst I ever did was shred my old boyfriend's favourite towel."

stronger than *tear* — implies destruction in many small pieces; common in both literal and figurative uses (*shred a document*, *shred someone's argument*).

freak out

phrasal verb C1

to suddenly become extremely upset, anxious, or angry; to lose control emotionally.

S1E1 — "I got freaked out, and it hit me: how much Barry looks like Mr. Potato Head."

the most common American-English way to describe a panic reaction — you will hear it constantly in everyday speech.

walk out on

phrasal verb C1

to abandon someone or end a relationship suddenly, leaving them behind.

S1E1 — Ross: "Ever since she walked out on me…"

stronger than *leave* — implies betrayal and finality. Central to Ross's storyline in this episode.

hit on

phrasal verb C1

to flirt with someone in an obvious, often unwelcome way.

S1E1 — Ross to Joey: "Stop hitting on her! It's her wedding day."

essential dating vocabulary in American English — the standard verb for unwanted romantic attention.

vulnerability

n C1

the state of being easily hurt emotionally or physically; openness to being attacked.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "Try not to let my vulnerability become a factor here."

a deliberately formal word Ross uses to be self-aware about his recent divorce — knowing it adds nuance to character moments.

upbeat

adj C1

cheerful and positive; optimistic in tone or mood.

S1E1 — Monica to Rachel: "You're surprisingly upbeat."

common in everyday conversation when describing mood — pairs well with its opposite *downbeat* / *down*.

drift apart

phrasal verb C1

to slowly become less close to someone over time, usually without a specific argument.

S1E1 — Rachel to Monica: "I know we've drifted apart…"

describes a kind of slow, quiet relationship breakdown — useful for talking about old friends or family.

murky

adj C1

unclear, dark, or hard to understand; literally cloudy/dim, figuratively confused or morally ambiguous.

S1E1 — Phoebe, after Ross tells her to stop cleansing his aura: "Fine, be murky."

versatile word — *murky water*, *murky details*, *murky past*. Stronger and more vivid than *unclear*.

hysterical

adj C1

uncontrollably emotional, especially crying or laughing; in informal use, also "extremely funny".

S1E1 — Ross: "So that hysterical phone call from a woman sobbing 'I'll never have grandchildren'…"

a near-daily word in spoken English with two distinct meanings (panicked / hilarious) — context decides which.

fixate on

phrasal verb C1

to focus obsessively on something, often to the exclusion of more important things.

S1E1 — Ross about Carol being a lesbian: "Why does everyone keep fixating on that?"

sharper than *focus on* — carries a sense of unhealthy obsession. Common in psychological and everyday talk.

whatsoever

adv C1

at all; used after a negative to add emphasis (= "of any kind").

S1E1 — Ross assembling furniture: "I see no worm guys whatsoever."

elegant emphasiser that immediately lifts your English — *no idea whatsoever*, *no reason whatsoever*.

steer clear of

idiom C1

to deliberately avoid someone or something, usually because they're dangerous or troublesome.

S1E1 — Chandler, after Phoebe says she once shredded a boyfriend's towel: "Steer clear of you."

more natural and idiomatic than *avoid* in spoken English. Comes from steering a boat away from rocks.

have a crush on

idiom C1

to feel a strong, often secret, romantic attraction to someone — usually short-lived or one-sided.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "In high school I had a major crush on you."

the standard English phrase for unspoken romantic feelings — essential dating vocabulary.

geeky

adj C1

awkwardly studious or nerdy; socially clumsy in a way associated with deep interest in niche topics.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "I figured you thought I was Monica's geeky brother."

very common in informal English, with a softer, more affectionate tone than *nerdy*.

get screwed

idiom C1

to be cheated, treated very unfairly, or end up with a terrible deal (informal).

S1E1 — Joey, hearing what Ross got from the divorce: "You got screwed."

widely-used informal idiom for being on the losing end of any deal — work, money, relationships.

live off

phrasal verb C1

to depend on someone or something for financial support; to survive using a particular resource.

S1E1 — Monica to Rachel: "You can't live off your parents."

the standard phrasal for financial dependence — also used literally (*live off bread and water*).

spell out

phrasal verb C1

to explain something in detail, often because the listener isn't getting the hint.

S1E1 — Joey to Paul: "What? You wanna spell it out with noodles?"

great for impatient or sarcastic contexts — *do I have to spell it out for you?* is everyday English.

get ahead of oneself

idiom C1

to act, plan, or assume too quickly, beyond what is reasonable for the current stage.

S1E1 — Rachel, when Phoebe offers her a used wedding dress after her first night with Paul: "I think we're getting a little ahead of ourselves."

polished idiom for politely slowing down a conversation or expectation — common in workplace and dating contexts.

take credit for

idiom C1

to claim recognition or praise for something — sometimes deservedly, sometimes not.

S1E1 — Monica about Paul's "two-year dry spell" story: "I take credit for Paul."

essential workplace and social vocabulary — pairs with its opposite *take the blame for*.

wondrous

adj C2

inspiring wonder or awe; remarkable in a beautiful, almost magical way (literary register).

S1E1 — Phoebe singing: "Love is a wondrous work of art."

an elevated, slightly old-fashioned synonym for *wonderful* — recognising it helps you read poetry, lyrics, and literary fiction.

dread

v C1

to feel deep fear or anxiety about something that is going to happen.

S1E2 — Rachel about returning Barry's ring: "Like I wasn't dreading tomorrow enough."

stronger and more emotional than *fear* or *worry about* — pairs naturally with future events (*dread Monday morning*, *dread the meeting*).

anecdote

n C1

a short, often personal story told to illustrate a point or amuse.

S1E2 — Mrs. Geller to Ross: "Any stories? No news, no little anecdotes to share with the folks?"

essential for sounding sophisticated in conversation and writing — *a funny anecdote*, *a personal anecdote*.

shoot for the stars

idiom C1

to set very high goals; to aim for extraordinary success.

S1E2 — Mrs. Geller: "People like Ross need to shoot for the stars."

classic American motivational idiom — common in interviews, speeches, and self-help contexts.

roll with the punches

idiom C1

to adapt calmly to difficult or unexpected situations rather than resisting them.

S1E2 — Carol about naming the baby: "Things change. Roll with the punches."

a boxing metaphor that's everyday English — shows resilience and flexibility. Useful in work and life contexts.

cheap shot

idiom C1

an unfair, mean, or low remark — often aimed at someone's weakness.

S1E2 — Rachel after insulting Mindy's looks on the phone: "That was a cheap shot, but I feel so much better now!"

comes from sports but used in all argument/debate contexts. Knowing it lets you spot when someone's playing dirty.

well up

phrasal verb C1

(of tears or emotion) to rise and fill the eyes; to be on the verge of crying.

S1E2 — Susan to Monica during the sonogram: "Are you welling up?"

the precise English phrasal for "almost crying" — more vivid than *getting emotional*.

stomp on

phrasal verb C1

to step on heavily; figuratively, to crush someone's feelings or hopes.

S1E2 — Rachel rehearsing for Barry: "I'm the girl that stomped on your heart."

very emotional figurative usage — *stomp on someone's dreams / heart / pride*.

latch on

phrasal verb C1

to fix attention on something obsessively; to grab onto an idea and not let go.

S1E2 — Monica about her mother bringing up lasagna: "He latches on."

describes obsessive focus — common in family/personality talk and tech (*users latched on to the new feature*).

fluff

v C1

to shake or pat something soft (a pillow, hair, feathers) to make it look fuller.

S1E2 — Chandler: "She already fluffed that pillow."

specific everyday verb — also figurative as a noun (*fluff* = meaningless filler content).

ammunition

n C1

literally bullets/shells; figuratively, facts or arguments used to attack someone.

S1E2 — Monica about her parents: "I just don't wanna give them any more ammunition."

the figurative sense is everywhere in political and personal arguments — *give the opposition ammunition*.

take the heat off

idiom C1

to deflect attention, blame, or pressure away from someone.

S1E2 — Monica to Ross about announcing Carol's pregnancy: "It might take the heat off of me."

essential office/family idiom — also flipped as *the heat is on* (= under pressure).

leave at the altar

idiom C1

to abandon a partner at their wedding, often by failing to show up; figuratively, to back out of any commitment at the last moment.

S1E2 — Mrs. Geller to Rachel: "At least she had the chance to leave a man at the altar."

vivid wedding-day idiom that extends to business deals (*left at the altar by the buyer*) and politics.

chubby

adj C1

slightly fat in a soft, often cute way — usually used of children, cheeks, or fingers.

S1E2 — Mr. Geller to Monica: "Even when you were a chubby kid you had no friends."

gentler than *fat*, more specific than *overweight* — common in everyday body descriptions.

dump

v C1

to end a romantic relationship abruptly, usually as the one who leaves.

S1E2 — Ross to Rachel: "as someone who's recently been dumped." / Rachel: "I dumped him."

the standard informal verb for breakups in American English — the active/passive contrast (*I dumped him* vs *I got dumped*) tells the whole story.

settle for

phrasal verb C1

to accept something less than what you really wanted, often reluctantly.

S1E2 — Rachel about her ring possibly going to Mindy: "Like she'd settle for that."

key relationship and career vocabulary — *don't settle for less*, *settle for a draw*.

border on

phrasal verb C1

to come very close to being a particular (usually negative) thing.

S1E2 — Ross about the proposed baby name Helen Willick Bunch Geller: "I think that borders on child abuse."

elegant hedge — lets you say something is *almost* X without fully committing. *Borders on rude / genius / absurd*.

driven

adj C1

highly motivated and determined to succeed, often to the point of obsession.

S1E2 — Phoebe describing her sister: "She's this high-powered, driven, career-type."

standard CV and character-description adjective in professional English — pairs with *ambitious*, *focused*, *high-achieving*.

tilt

v C1

to move something into a sloping position; to tip or lean.

S1E2 — Chandler at the sonogram: "If you tilt your head and relax your eyes, it kind of looks like an old potato."

precise physical verb — used for heads, cameras, balance, and figuratively for opinion shifts (*the polls have tilted left*).

cushion the blow

idiom C1

to soften the impact of bad news or a painful experience.

S1E3 — Joey explaining dating euphemisms: "Cushions the blow. Like your parents putting the dog to sleep and saying it went to a farm."

elegant idiom for breaking bad news gently — common in HR, medicine, journalism, relationships.

minion

n C1

a low-ranking follower or servant of a powerful person — often used humorously or with disdain.

S1E3 — Phoebe about the bank crediting her too much money: "Satan's minions at work again!"

very common in fantasy, politics, and office sarcasm (*the boss's minions*). Took on extra cultural weight from the Despicable Me films.

karmic

adj C2

relating to karma; reflecting the moral consequences of past actions.

S1E3 — Phoebe about spending money that isn't hers: "It'd be this giant karmic debt."

signals an introspective, slightly spiritual register — common in modern English to talk about cause-and-effect outside religion.

pick off

phrasal verb C1

to remove or eliminate one at a time, often by targeting the weakest.

S1E3 — Rachel about her friends judging her dates: "Coyotes picking off the weak members of the herd."

evocative phrasal — used literally (snipers, predators) and figuratively (competitors, job candidates).

odds

n C1

the probability or likelihood that something will happen; the ratio of chances for vs. against.

S1E3 — Monica to Rachel: "The odds of that happening are slimmer if they never get to meet the guy."

essential vocabulary for probability, gambling, and forecasting — *the odds are good/against you*, *beat the odds*.

whimper

v C1

to make low, weak crying or complaining sounds — like a hurt animal.

S1E3 — Ross to Monica about losing the dog: "Can't I whimper a little bit?"

more specific than *cry* — implies quiet, pathetic distress. Also famous from T.S. Eliot: *"Not with a bang but a whimper."*

crooked

adj C1

bent or twisted out of shape; also figuratively dishonest or corrupt.

S1E3 — Phoebe praising Alan: "His smile was crooked. Like the man in the shoe."

two distinct uses — physical (*a crooked nose*) and moral (*a crooked politician*). Both very common.

yardstick

n C2

a standard or measure used to judge or compare; figuratively, a benchmark.

S1E3 — Rachel about Alan: "He is the yardstick by which future boyfriends will be measured."

formal/literary metaphor — common in essays, reviews, and management talk. Pairs with *benchmark*, *gold standard*.

innate

adj C2

present from birth; existing as a natural, inherent quality.

S1E3 — Chandler being absurdly sincere about Alan: "It's his innate 'Alan-ness' that we adore."

academic and psychological register — *innate talent*, *innate ability*, *innate goodness*. Opposite of *learned* or *acquired*.

flaw

n C1

an imperfection or weakness — in an object, plan, character, or argument.

S1E3 — Chandler defending his smoking: "So I have a flaw! Big deal!"

core vocabulary for criticism — *a fatal flaw*, *a character flaw*, *a flaw in the design*.

snort

v C1

to make a sudden, harsh sound through the nose, often when laughing or in disgust.

S1E3 — Chandler listing his friends' annoying habits: "Monica, who snorts when she laughs."

very specific sound-verb — also used for animals (*the horse snorted*) and as a noun (*a snort of laughter*).

endearing

adj C2

inspiring affection; charming in a sweet, sometimes clumsy way.

S1E3 — Phoebe about Joey's knuckle-cracking: "I think it's endearing."

the precise word for "annoying-but-cute" — often used to describe quirks, accents, or small habits in loved ones.

bottom line

idiom C1

the most important fact or final conclusion; figuratively the core point of an argument.

S1E3 — Chandler defiantly: "The bottom line is, smoking is cool, and you know it."

essential business and conversation phrase — *what's the bottom line?* cuts straight to the point.

déjà vu

n C1

the strange feeling that you have already experienced the current moment.

S1E3 — Phoebe: "Wait! I'm getting a déjà vu. No, I'm not."

borrowed from French and fully naturalized in English — used both literally (the eerie feeling) and figuratively (*it felt like déjà vu*).

let one's guard down

idiom C1

to relax one's emotional defences; to become vulnerable after being cautious.

S1E3 — Alan to Monica after the breakup: "You let your guard down. You start to care about someone, and…"

boxing metaphor for emotional risk — central to relationship and therapy talk.

can't stand

idiom C1

to strongly dislike; to be unable to tolerate someone or something.

S1E3 — Alan after breaking up: "I had a great time with you. I just can't stand your friends."

the most common informal way to express strong dislike in English — stronger than *don't like*, softer than *hate*.

gloves come off

idiom C2

the point at which people stop being polite and start fighting seriously — verbal or otherwise.

S1E3 — Chandler narrating after Monica snaps back: "The hair comes out and the gloves come off."

boxing-derived idiom signaling escalation — common in politics, debates, sports, and arguments. Often paired with *take the gloves off*.

omnipotent

adj C2

having unlimited power; able to do anything (often used of gods, rulers, or in thought experiments).

S1E4 — Phoebe to the group: "If I were omnipotent for a day, I would want world peace…"

elevated register word from theology and philosophy — useful in essays and abstract discussion. Pairs with *omniscient* (all-knowing) and *omnipresent* (everywhere).

theoretical

adj C1

based on ideas or theory rather than practical experience; hypothetical.

S1E4 — Phoebe to Joey: "Ross, I'm sorry. I thought it was a theoretical question."

academic and everyday word — *a theoretical possibility*, *purely theoretical*. Often contrasted with *practical* or *applied*.

nod off

phrasal verb C1

to fall asleep accidentally, usually for a short time and while sitting up.

S1E4 — Monica to Phoebe in the coffee shop: "You just nodded off again."

the precise English phrasal for unintentional dozing — common in commutes, meetings, lectures. Softer than *fall asleep*.

reassure

v C1

to remove someone's doubts or fears by telling them everything is okay.

S1E4 — Phoebe about her grandmother and the boyfriend: "They reassure each other that they're having a good time."

standard professional and emotional vocabulary — *the doctor reassured the patient*, *let me reassure you*. Pairs with *reassurance*.

consummate

v C2

to complete a marriage or relationship by having sex for the first time; more broadly, to make a deal complete or final.

S1E4 — Ross: "Today's the day Carol and I first consummated our physical relationship."

formal/legal register — appears in business contexts too (*consummate a merger*, *consummate the deal*). Knowing the register prevents accidental comedy.

pass on

phrasal verb C1

to decline something offered; to skip it.

S1E4 — Ross to Joey and Chandler: "I better pass on the game."

polite, common way to refuse an offer — *I'll pass on dessert*, *pass on the meeting*. Also means "transmit to someone else" (*pass on the message*).

take one's mind off

idiom C1

to distract oneself from something worrying or upsetting.

S1E4 — Ross agreeing to come to hockey: "Maybe it will take my mind off it."

essential emotional-coping vocabulary — *take your mind off work*, *take your mind off the breakup*.

phenomenal

adj C1

extraordinary or remarkable, especially in degree.

S1E4 — Rachel to her friend: "Kiki, you look phenomenal."

strong, slightly formal alternative to *amazing* — works for performance, looks, growth, success. Pairs with *phenomenally well*.

dirt

n C1

(informal) gossip or scandalous information about someone.

S1E4 — Rachel's friends to her: "Tell me all the dirt."

colloquial second meaning of a common word — *got any dirt on him?*, *digging up dirt*. Common in journalism and casual talk.

predicament

n C2

a difficult, unpleasant, or embarrassing situation that's hard to escape.

S1E4 — Chandler in the ER: "It's an emergency. But you know that, or we'd be in the predicament room."

elegant, slightly bookish word for *trouble* or *mess* — *find oneself in a predicament*, *a moral predicament*.

bonehead

n C1

(informal) a stupid person; someone who makes obvious mistakes.

S1E4 — Chandler hitting himself: "Bonehead! Bonehead!"

softer than *idiot* — used affectionately or in self-criticism. Pairs with the adjective *boneheaded* (= a *boneheaded mistake*).

bring down

phrasal verb C1

to make someone feel sad or depressed; to lower someone's mood.

S1E4 — Monica to her friends: "I didn't mean to bring you down."

essential emotional vocabulary — *don't let it bring you down*. Also has other senses (*bring down a government*, *bring down prices*).

stunning

adj C1

extremely impressive, beautiful, or shocking.

S1E4 — Chandler about George Stephanopoulos: "Was he wearing a stunning blue suit?"

versatile high-praise adjective — works for views, outfits, news, performance. Also literal: *a stunning blow* (= one that stuns).

draw out

phrasal verb C1

to encourage a quiet or shy person to talk or express themselves.

S1E4 — Monica about George: "You have to draw him out. And then when you do, he's a preppy animal."

standard social/therapeutic vocabulary — *draw out a shy child*, *draw out the truth*. Also means literally "extend" (*a long-drawn-out meeting*).

repressed

adj C2

holding back feelings, especially emotional or sexual ones, in an unhealthy way.

S1E4 — Rachel describing Chandler: "Chandler's one of those repressed guys who you'd have to seduce."

psychology-derived but mainstream — *emotionally repressed*, *repressed memory*, *repressed anger*. Knows when something isn't said.

seduce

v C1

to attract someone sexually; more broadly, to tempt or persuade by appeal.

S1E4 — Rachel about Chandler: "you'd have to seduce" him.

strong sexual register, but the figurative sense is common in marketing and politics — *seduced by an idea*, *seduced by the promise of money*.

tender

adj C1

gentle, loving, and considerate; also literally soft or easily damaged.

S1E4 — Monica about Joey: "He helped me put on my coat and he was really tender."

key word for emotional warmth in writing — *a tender moment*, *tender kiss*. Also food (*tender steak*) and pain (*tender to the touch*).

roughhousing

n C2

noisy, rough, physical play — often between children or young men.

S1E4 — ER staff to Ross and Joey: "No roughhousing in my ER!"

very specific American word for play-fighting — useful for talking about kids, sports, dorm life. Also a verb: *roughhouse*.

dent

n C1

a hollow or depression made by pressure or a blow; figuratively, a small amount of progress on a big problem.

S1E4 — Chandler complaining about his injury: "My face is dented."

common literal use (car dents, dented can) and rich figurative use — *make a dent in the workload*, *put a dent in the budget*.

maneuver

n C1

a skilful or clever movement or action, often requiring planning.

S1E5 — Chandler marvelling at bra removal: "You reach in, there's one maneuver and bam!"

used physically (driving, sports) and figuratively (politics, business — *a clever maneuver*). British spelling: *manoeuvre*.

rot in hell

idiom C1

a bitter curse wishing lasting suffering on someone you despise.

S1E5 — Phoebe about an ex: "Except for Kevin Millmore, may he rot in hell."

strong, dramatic expression of contempt — common in film, argument, and dark humour. Knowing its weight stops you using it casually.

gang up on

phrasal verb C1

to join with others to attack, criticise, or pressure one person.

S1E5 — Chandler about breaking up with Janice together: "She'd feel like we're ganging up on her."

core social-dynamics vocabulary — *don't gang up on me*, *they ganged up on the new kid*. Implies unfair numbers.

burn someone up

idiom C1

(informal) to make someone very angry or annoyed.

S1E5 — Rachel about her father: "That guy, he burns me up."

vivid informal anger idiom — distinct from literal burning. Pairs with *that really burns me up*.

eager

adj C1

keen and impatient to do or have something; full of enthusiasm.

S1E5 — Joey before approaching Angela: "I don't wanna seem too eager."

key word for describing motivation — *eager to please*, *eager to learn*. Pairs with the noun phrase *an eager beaver*.

accent

v C1

to emphasise or draw attention to a feature; to make something stand out.

S1E5 — Angela: "I'm wearing a dress that accents my boobs."

the verb sense (≠ the noun "accent" = pronunciation) is common in fashion, design, and cooking — *accent the waist*, *accented with gold*.

glamour

n C1

an attractive, exciting quality of luxury, beauty, or sophistication.

S1E5 — Rachel sarcastically: "Big glamour night. Me and Monica at Launderama."

evocative noun with adjective *glamorous* — used for celebrities, fashion, travel. Note the British -our spelling kept even in American English.

freakish

adj C1

very strange, abnormal, or unusual to the point of being startling.

S1E5 — Joey: "You wanna hear a freakish coincidence?"

stronger than *strange* — *a freakish accident*, *freakishly tall*. Related to *freak* (already known) but more formal in tone.

sophisticated

adj C1

worldly, refined, and culturally aware; also (of systems) complex and advanced.

S1E5 — Angela describing Bob: "He's smart, sophisticated, and he has a real job."

dual use — describing people (*a sophisticated palate*) and technology (*a sophisticated algorithm*). Essential review/writing vocabulary.

belch

v C1

to noisily release air from the stomach through the mouth; to burp.

S1E5 — Monica about a bad date: "your cousin who could belch the alphabet."

more formal/literary than *burp* — also figurative (*the factory belched smoke*). Knowing both registers is useful.

one of a kind

idiom C1

unique; the only one of its type, with no equal.

S1E5 — Joey about Angela: "She's like one of a kind, like a snowflake."

common compliment and marketing phrase — *a one-of-a-kind opportunity*, *truly one of a kind*. Hyphenated when used before a noun.

fuzzy

adj C1

covered in soft short hairs or fibres; also blurred, unclear, or imprecise.

S1E5 — Joey defending his fabric softener: "like a little fuzzy bear."

two senses — physical (*a fuzzy sweater*) and abstract (*fuzzy logic*, *the details are fuzzy*). Both very common.

supremely

adv C2

to the highest possible degree; extremely.

S1E5 — Janice: "I had the most supremely awful day."

strong intensifier with a slightly formal/dramatic flavour — *supremely confident*, *supremely talented*. Lifts your register above *very*.

blow off

phrasal verb C1

(informal) to deliberately ignore or skip something, or to treat someone dismissively.

S1E5 — Janice: "I blew off the afternoon. I went shopping."

very common American phrasal — *blow off a meeting*, *blow off steam* (different sense), *he blew me off*. Signals casual disregard.

brutal

adj C1

savagely violent; also used informally for anything harsh, painful, or unpleasant.

S1E5 — Joey about Chandler's hug with Janice: "the hug looked pretty brutal."

hugely versatile in modern English — *a brutal workout*, *brutal honesty*, *brutally cold*. The informal "very tough" sense is everywhere.

judgment call

idiom C1

a decision based on personal opinion rather than fixed rules, because no clear answer exists.

S1E5 — Ross on which load white cotton panties belong in: "That would be a judgment call."

essential workplace and everyday phrase — *I had to make a judgment call*. Signals discretion under uncertainty.

rambunctious

adj C2

noisy, energetic, and difficult to control; boisterous in a playful way.

S1E5 — Joey to Monica: "Like you've never got a little rambunctious with Ross."

colourful, slightly literary adjective for wild energy — *rambunctious kids*, *a rambunctious crowd*. Marks an advanced vocabulary.

grieve

v C1

to feel or express deep sorrow, especially over a loss or death.

S1E5 — Joey explaining Chandler's absence: "He needed time to grieve."

the precise verb for processing loss — *grieve for/over someone*. Related noun *grief* and adjective *grief-stricken*. Used literally and humorously.

impending

adj C2

about to happen soon, usually something bad or threatening.

S1E6 — Ross at Joey's play: "Notice, no fear. No sense of impending doom."

formal, ominous word almost always paired with negatives — *impending doom*, *impending disaster*, *impending deadline*. Marks advanced register.

out of one's league

idiom C1

too good, attractive, or skilled for someone to realistically obtain or compete with.

S1E6 — Chandler about the woman in the café: "Could she be more out of my league?"

sports-derived dating and competition idiom — *she's out of my league*, *playing out of his league*. Extremely common in spoken English.

sap

n C2

(informal) a foolish, gullible, or overly sentimental person who is easily fooled.

S1E6 — the talent agency's note to Joey: "Loved your work. Call me a sap."

old-fashioned but still-used insult — *don't be a sap*, *the poor sap believed her*. Note the unrelated literal sense (tree sap).

make out

phrasal verb C1

to kiss and embrace passionately; (separately) to manage or to perceive with difficulty.

S1E6 — Estelle: "I wanna go to the top of the Empire State Building and make out."

very common American phrasal — but watch the other senses: *how did you make out?* (= manage), *I can't make out the sign* (= see clearly).

widowed

adj C1

having lost one's spouse to death and not remarried.

S1E6 — Chandler probing Aurora's marital status: "So you're widowed? Hopefully?"

precise family-status vocabulary — *a widowed mother*, *recently widowed*. Related nouns *widow* (woman) and *widower* (man).

twisted

adj C1

mentally or morally perverse; strange in a disturbing, unhealthy way.

S1E6 — Ross about Aurora's love life: "It's twisted!"

strong character judgement — *a twisted mind*, *twisted humour*, *a twisted plot*. Distinct from the literal "bent" meaning.

monogamy

n C2

the practice of having only one romantic or sexual partner at a time.

S1E6 — Phoebe: "monogamy can be a tricky concept… anthropologically speaking."

academic and relationship register — pairs with its opposites *polygamy* and *non-monogamy*. Adjective: *monogamous*.

put forth

phrasal verb C2

to propose or present an idea, theory, or argument for consideration.

S1E6 — Phoebe: "There's a theory put forth by Richard Leakey…"

formal/academic phrasal common in essays and debate — *put forth a proposal*, *put forth an argument*. More elevated than *suggest*.

madcap

adj C2

amusingly reckless, zany, or wildly impulsive.

S1E6 — Phoebe teasing Monica: "All right, you madcap gal!"

playful, slightly literary adjective for chaotic energy — *a madcap scheme*, *madcap adventure*. Often used ironically (as Phoebe does here).

condensation

n C1

tiny drops of water that form when warm moist air touches a cold surface.

S1E6 — Monica imagining her own anxiety: "Beads of condensation are inching closer to the wood."

precise science/everyday word — *condensation on the window*. The verb is *condense*; also a chemistry and writing term (*a condensed summary*).

big break

idiom C1

a sudden opportunity that leads to major success, especially in a career.

S1E6 — Joey about the Pacino movie: "This is a big break for me!"

core showbiz and career idiom — *get your big break*, *waiting for a big break*. Knowing it helps you talk about luck and opportunity.

rogue

adj C1

behaving unexpectedly or out of control; separate from the normal group.

S1E6 — Joey looking for tweezers: "I think I have a rogue hair."

richly used in modern English — *a rogue agent*, *go rogue*, *a rogue wave*. Implies something acting on its own, against the rules.

resentment

n C1

a feeling of bitter anger or ill-will at being treated unfairly.

S1E6 — Chandler to Ross: "Do I sense resentment?"

key emotional-vocabulary noun — *harbour resentment*, *deep resentment*. The verb is *resent* (= *I resent that remark*).

clench

v C1

to close or tighten tightly, especially fists, teeth, or muscles.

S1E6 — the film director correcting Joey's butt acting: "No, that was clenching."

precise physical verb for tension — *clench your fists/jaw*, *a clenched smile*. Conveys anger, fear, or effort in writing.

unattainable

adj C2

impossible to reach, achieve, or obtain.

S1E6 — Ross describing Aurora: "this woman was unbelievably sexy and beautiful, intelligent, unattainable."

formal antonym of *attainable* — *an unattainable goal*, *unattainable standards*. Common in discussions of ambition and self-image.

strewn

v C2

scattered or spread untidily over an area (past participle of *strew*).

S1E6 — Monica mocking the abandoned shoes: "casually strewn about in that reckless, haphazard manner."

literary, slightly formal verb — *clothes strewn across the floor*, *a path strewn with leaves*. Adds vividness to descriptive writing.

haphazard

adj C2

lacking order or planning; done randomly or carelessly.

S1E6 — Monica, sarcastically: "in that reckless, haphazard manner."

precise critique word — *a haphazard approach*, *haphazardly arranged*. Stronger and more specific than *messy* or *random*.

reckless

adj C1

acting without thinking about danger or consequences.

S1E6 — Monica about the shoes: "in that reckless, haphazard manner."

core safety/judgement word — *reckless driving*, *reckless spending*, *reckless disregard*. Has legal weight too (*reckless endangerment*).

blow it

idiom C1

to fail at something by making a mistake; to waste a good opportunity.

S1E6 — Joey after being fired: "I finally get my shot, and I blow it!"

extremely common informal idiom for failure — *don't blow it*, *I really blew it*. Pairs naturally with *shot* / *chance* / *opportunity*.

blackout

n C1

a period when electrical power fails over an area; also a temporary loss of consciousness or memory.

S1E7 — Joey: "The entire city's blacked out." (the episode is "The One With The Blackout")

multiple senses worth knowing — *a power blackout*, *a media blackout*, *I had a blackout* (memory loss). The verb form is *black out*.

eligible

adj C1

qualified or allowed to do/have something; (of a person) considered a desirable partner.

S1E7 — Mrs. Geller worrying who Monica might meet: "Eligible looters?"

key formal word — *eligible to vote*, *eligible for benefits*, *an eligible bachelor*. Pairs with the noun *eligibility*.

vestibule

n C2

a small entrance hall or lobby just inside the outer door of a building.

S1E7 — Chandler stuck with Jill Goodacre: "I am trapped in an ATM vestibule."

precise architectural word that sounds sophisticated — also used in anatomy (*the vestibule of the ear*). Chandler's debate over *vestibule* vs *atrium* is the joke.

officiate

v C2

to perform the duties of an official, especially to lead a ceremony or referee a game.

S1E7 — Joey lighting candles: "Officiating at tonight's blackout is Rabbi Tribbiani."

formal ceremony vocabulary — *officiate a wedding*, *officiate the match*. The person is an *official* or *officiant*.

overrated

adj C1

considered better or more important than it really is.

S1E7 — Ross consoling Rachel: "Passion is way overrated."

essential opinion word — *an overrated film*, *wildly overrated*. The opposite is *underrated*. Common in reviews and casual debate.

burn out

phrasal verb C1

to lose energy, intensity, or fuel until nothing is left; (of people) to collapse from overwork.

S1E7 — Ross about passion: "Eventually, it kind of burns out."

widely used for relationships, fires, and especially work stress — *burned out at work*, *burnout* (the noun) is a major modern term.

miss out on

phrasal verb C1

to lose a chance to do or experience something good.

S1E7 — Ross: "For those people who miss out on that passion thing, there's other stuff."

core FOMO-era vocabulary — *don't miss out*, *miss out on the fun*. Always takes *on* before the thing missed.

friend zone

idiom C1

a situation where one person wants romance but the other sees them only as a friend.

S1E7 — Joey to Ross about Rachel: "You're in the 'friend zone'… you're mayor of the zone."

modern dating idiom now fully mainstream — *stuck in the friend zone*, *friend-zoned*. Useful and very current.

lay the groundwork

idiom C2

to do the preparatory work that makes future success possible.

S1E7 — Ross defending his slow approach: "I'm laying the groundwork."

polished professional idiom — *lay the groundwork for a deal*, *the groundwork for reform*. Signals patient, strategic preparation.

tousle

v C2

to make hair or fabric untidy by ruffling it, often affectionately.

S1E7 — Ross distinguishing affectionate touches: "this is a tousle. That was no tousle."

vivid, slightly literary verb — *tousled hair* is a common style description in fashion and fiction. Rare enough to mark advanced vocabulary.

carcass

n C2

the dead body of an animal, especially one prepared for meat.

S1E7 — Chandler's mental note: "If she offers you mangled animal carcass, you take it!"

precise word for an animal corpse (a human one is a *corpse*) — also figurative (*the carcass of the old factory*). Common in cooking and nature writing.

trample

v C1

to tread heavily on something and crush or damage it; figuratively, to disregard others' rights.

S1E7 — Ross worrying about the cat in the hall: "She'll get trampled."

literal (*trampled by the crowd*) and figurative (*trample on someone's rights*). Strong, physical verb common in news and protest contexts.

damage control

idiom C1

action taken to limit the harm caused by a mistake, accident, or scandal.

S1E7 — Chandler to himself after an awkward line: "Damage control. Get back to the gum."

essential PR, politics, and everyday phrase — *do damage control*, *in damage-control mode*. Knowing it helps you follow news and corporate language.

loathe

v C2

to hate or dislike something intensely.

S1E7 — Chandler cringing at himself: "I loathe myself!"

much stronger than *dislike* and more formal than *hate* — *I loathe Mondays*. Related adjective *loathsome* and noun *loathing* (*self-loathing*).

impish

adj C2

playfully mischievous in a charming way.

S1E7 — Chandler psyching himself up to blow a bubble: "It's got a boyish charm. It's impish."

delightful character adjective — *an impish grin*, *impish humour*. From *imp* (a small mischievous creature), which appears in the very next line.

superficial

adj C1

concerned only with the surface; shallow, lacking depth.

S1E7 — Rachel admitting her attraction to Paolo: "I know it's superficial, and we have nothing in common."

key word for critique — *a superficial analysis*, *superficial charm*, *superficial wounds* (= not deep). Pairs with *shallow* and opposes *profound*.

definitive

adj C2

the most authoritative, complete, or final of its kind; settling all doubt.

S1E7 — Joey: "I have the definitive one." (the best blackout story)

strong academic/review word — *the definitive guide*, *a definitive answer*, *definitive proof*. Don't confuse with *definite* (= certain).

spooky

adj C1

strange and frightening in a slightly supernatural way; eerie.

S1E7 — looking in the dark fridge during the blackout: "Spooky." / later "Kind of spooky without any lights."

common informal word for eerie atmospheres — *a spooky old house*, *spooky coincidence*. Lighter than *terrifying*; often playful.

idiom C1

to obstruct or interfere with something's progress.

S1E7 — Ross warning Paolo: "if you get in the way of us becoming a thing, then I would be very sad."

everyday obstruction idiom — *don't let fear get in the way of your dreams*, *work got in the way*. Extremely common in spoken English.
