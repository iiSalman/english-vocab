blackout

n C1

a period when electrical power fails over an area; also a temporary loss of consciousness or memory.

S1E7 — Joey: "The entire city's blacked out." (the episode is "The One With The Blackout")

multiple senses worth knowing — *a power blackout*, *a media blackout*, *I had a blackout* (memory loss). The verb form is *black out*.

eligible

adj C1

qualified or allowed to do/have something; (of a person) considered a desirable partner.

S1E7 — Mrs. Geller worrying who Monica might meet: "Eligible looters?"

key formal word — *eligible to vote*, *eligible for benefits*, *an eligible bachelor*. Pairs with the noun *eligibility*.

vestibule

n C2

a small entrance hall or lobby just inside the outer door of a building.

S1E7 — Chandler stuck with Jill Goodacre: "I am trapped in an ATM vestibule."

precise architectural word that sounds sophisticated — also used in anatomy (*the vestibule of the ear*). Chandler's debate over *vestibule* vs *atrium* is the joke.

officiate

v C2

to perform the duties of an official, especially to lead a ceremony or referee a game.

S1E7 — Joey lighting candles: "Officiating at tonight's blackout is Rabbi Tribbiani."

formal ceremony vocabulary — *officiate a wedding*, *officiate the match*. The person is an *official* or *officiant*.

overrated

adj C1

considered better or more important than it really is.

S1E7 — Ross consoling Rachel: "Passion is way overrated."

essential opinion word — *an overrated film*, *wildly overrated*. The opposite is *underrated*. Common in reviews and casual debate.

burn out

phrasal verb C1

to lose energy, intensity, or fuel until nothing is left; (of people) to collapse from overwork.

S1E7 — Ross about passion: "Eventually, it kind of burns out."

widely used for relationships, fires, and especially work stress — *burned out at work*, *burnout* (the noun) is a major modern term.

miss out on

phrasal verb C1

to lose a chance to do or experience something good.

S1E7 — Ross: "For those people who miss out on that passion thing, there's other stuff."

core FOMO-era vocabulary — *don't miss out*, *miss out on the fun*. Always takes *on* before the thing missed.

friend zone

idiom C1

a situation where one person wants romance but the other sees them only as a friend.

S1E7 — Joey to Ross about Rachel: "You're in the 'friend zone'… you're mayor of the zone."

modern dating idiom now fully mainstream — *stuck in the friend zone*, *friend-zoned*. Useful and very current.

lay the groundwork

idiom C2

to do the preparatory work that makes future success possible.

S1E7 — Ross defending his slow approach: "I'm laying the groundwork."

polished professional idiom — *lay the groundwork for a deal*, *the groundwork for reform*. Signals patient, strategic preparation.

tousle

v C2

to make hair or fabric untidy by ruffling it, often affectionately.

S1E7 — Ross distinguishing affectionate touches: "this is a tousle. That was no tousle."

vivid, slightly literary verb — *tousled hair* is a common style description in fashion and fiction. Rare enough to mark advanced vocabulary.

carcass

n C2

the dead body of an animal, especially one prepared for meat.

S1E7 — Chandler's mental note: "If she offers you mangled animal carcass, you take it!"

precise word for an animal corpse (a human one is a *corpse*) — also figurative (*the carcass of the old factory*). Common in cooking and nature writing.

trample

v C1

to tread heavily on something and crush or damage it; figuratively, to disregard others' rights.

S1E7 — Ross worrying about the cat in the hall: "She'll get trampled."

literal (*trampled by the crowd*) and figurative (*trample on someone's rights*). Strong, physical verb common in news and protest contexts.

damage control

idiom C1

action taken to limit the harm caused by a mistake, accident, or scandal.

S1E7 — Chandler to himself after an awkward line: "Damage control. Get back to the gum."

essential PR, politics, and everyday phrase — *do damage control*, *in damage-control mode*. Knowing it helps you follow news and corporate language.

loathe

v C2

to hate or dislike something intensely.

S1E7 — Chandler cringing at himself: "I loathe myself!"

much stronger than *dislike* and more formal than *hate* — *I loathe Mondays*. Related adjective *loathsome* and noun *loathing* (*self-loathing*).

impish

adj C2

playfully mischievous in a charming way.

S1E7 — Chandler psyching himself up to blow a bubble: "It's got a boyish charm. It's impish."

delightful character adjective — *an impish grin*, *impish humour*. From *imp* (a small mischievous creature), which appears in the very next line.

superficial

adj C1

concerned only with the surface; shallow, lacking depth.

S1E7 — Rachel admitting her attraction to Paolo: "I know it's superficial, and we have nothing in common."

key word for critique — *a superficial analysis*, *superficial charm*, *superficial wounds* (= not deep). Pairs with *shallow* and opposes *profound*.

definitive

adj C2

the most authoritative, complete, or final of its kind; settling all doubt.

S1E7 — Joey: "I have the definitive one." (the best blackout story)

strong academic/review word — *the definitive guide*, *a definitive answer*, *definitive proof*. Don't confuse with *definite* (= certain).

spooky

adj C1

strange and frightening in a slightly supernatural way; eerie.

S1E7 — looking in the dark fridge during the blackout: "Spooky." / later "Kind of spooky without any lights."

common informal word for eerie atmospheres — *a spooky old house*, *spooky coincidence*. Lighter than *terrifying*; often playful.

get in the way of

idiom C1

to obstruct or interfere with something's progress.

S1E7 — Ross warning Paolo: "if you get in the way of us becoming a thing, then I would be very sad."

everyday obstruction idiom — *don't let fear get in the way of your dreams*, *work got in the way*. Extremely common in spoken English.
