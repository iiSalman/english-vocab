metaphor

n C1

a way of describing something by saying it is something else, to make a comparison.

S1E1 — Rachel: "What if I want to be a purse? Or a hat? No, I don't want you to buy me a hat! It's a metaphor, Daddy!"

the joke of the scene depends on knowing this word — her father takes the comparison literally.

matrimony

n C2

the state of being married; formal word for marriage, common in ceremonies.

S1E1 — "We are gathered here to join Joanie Louise Cunningham and Charles in the bonds of holy matrimony."

the kind of elevated vocabulary you hear at weddings — recognising it helps you follow formal English in films and ceremonies.

revelation

n C1

the act of making something previously secret known; a surprising piece of information.

S1E1 — "It's more of a fifth-date kind of revelation."

Paul uses it to soften an awkward confession — the word's slightly formal weight makes the moment funnier.

shred

v C1

to tear or cut something into very small pieces.

S1E1 — "The worst I ever did was shred my old boyfriend's favourite towel."

stronger than *tear* — implies destruction in many small pieces; common in both literal and figurative uses (*shred a document*, *shred someone's argument*).

freak out

phrasal verb C1

to suddenly become extremely upset, anxious, or angry; to lose control emotionally.

S1E1 — "I got freaked out, and it hit me: how much Barry looks like Mr. Potato Head."

the most common American-English way to describe a panic reaction — you will hear it constantly in everyday speech.

walk out on

phrasal verb C1

to abandon someone or end a relationship suddenly, leaving them behind.

S1E1 — Ross: "Ever since she walked out on me…"

stronger than *leave* — implies betrayal and finality. Central to Ross's storyline in this episode.

hit on

phrasal verb C1

to flirt with someone in an obvious, often unwelcome way.

S1E1 — Ross to Joey: "Stop hitting on her! It's her wedding day."

essential dating vocabulary in American English — the standard verb for unwanted romantic attention.

vulnerability

n C1

the state of being easily hurt emotionally or physically; openness to being attacked.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "Try not to let my vulnerability become a factor here."

a deliberately formal word Ross uses to be self-aware about his recent divorce — knowing it adds nuance to character moments.

upbeat

adj C1

cheerful and positive; optimistic in tone or mood.

S1E1 — Monica to Rachel: "You're surprisingly upbeat."

common in everyday conversation when describing mood — pairs well with its opposite *downbeat* / *down*.

drift apart

phrasal verb C1

to slowly become less close to someone over time, usually without a specific argument.

S1E1 — Rachel to Monica: "I know we've drifted apart…"

describes a kind of slow, quiet relationship breakdown — useful for talking about old friends or family.

murky

adj C1

unclear, dark, or hard to understand; literally cloudy/dim, figuratively confused or morally ambiguous.

S1E1 — Phoebe, after Ross tells her to stop cleansing his aura: "Fine, be murky."

versatile word — *murky water*, *murky details*, *murky past*. Stronger and more vivid than *unclear*.

hysterical

adj C1

uncontrollably emotional, especially crying or laughing; in informal use, also "extremely funny".

S1E1 — Ross: "So that hysterical phone call from a woman sobbing 'I'll never have grandchildren'…"

a near-daily word in spoken English with two distinct meanings (panicked / hilarious) — context decides which.

fixate on

phrasal verb C1

to focus obsessively on something, often to the exclusion of more important things.

S1E1 — Ross about Carol being a lesbian: "Why does everyone keep fixating on that?"

sharper than *focus on* — carries a sense of unhealthy obsession. Common in psychological and everyday talk.

whatsoever

adv C1

at all; used after a negative to add emphasis (= "of any kind").

S1E1 — Ross assembling furniture: "I see no worm guys whatsoever."

elegant emphasiser that immediately lifts your English — *no idea whatsoever*, *no reason whatsoever*.

steer clear of

idiom C1

to deliberately avoid someone or something, usually because they're dangerous or troublesome.

S1E1 — Chandler, after Phoebe says she once shredded a boyfriend's towel: "Steer clear of you."

more natural and idiomatic than *avoid* in spoken English. Comes from steering a boat away from rocks.

have a crush on

idiom C1

to feel a strong, often secret, romantic attraction to someone — usually short-lived or one-sided.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "In high school I had a major crush on you."

the standard English phrase for unspoken romantic feelings — essential dating vocabulary.

geeky

adj C1

awkwardly studious or nerdy; socially clumsy in a way associated with deep interest in niche topics.

S1E1 — Ross to Rachel: "I figured you thought I was Monica's geeky brother."

very common in informal English, with a softer, more affectionate tone than *nerdy*.

get screwed

idiom C1

to be cheated, treated very unfairly, or end up with a terrible deal (informal).

S1E1 — Joey, hearing what Ross got from the divorce: "You got screwed."

widely-used informal idiom for being on the losing end of any deal — work, money, relationships.

live off

phrasal verb C1

to depend on someone or something for financial support; to survive using a particular resource.

S1E1 — Monica to Rachel: "You can't live off your parents."

the standard phrasal for financial dependence — also used literally (*live off bread and water*).

spell out

phrasal verb C1

to explain something in detail, often because the listener isn't getting the hint.

S1E1 — Joey to Paul: "What? You wanna spell it out with noodles?"

great for impatient or sarcastic contexts — *do I have to spell it out for you?* is everyday English.

get ahead of oneself

idiom C1

to act, plan, or assume too quickly, beyond what is reasonable for the current stage.

S1E1 — Rachel, when Phoebe offers her a used wedding dress after her first night with Paul: "I think we're getting a little ahead of ourselves."

polished idiom for politely slowing down a conversation or expectation — common in workplace and dating contexts.

take credit for

idiom C1

to claim recognition or praise for something — sometimes deservedly, sometimes not.

S1E1 — Monica about Paul's "two-year dry spell" story: "I take credit for Paul."

essential workplace and social vocabulary — pairs with its opposite *take the blame for*.

wondrous

adj C2

inspiring wonder or awe; remarkable in a beautiful, almost magical way (literary register).

S1E1 — Phoebe singing: "Love is a wondrous work of art."

an elevated, slightly old-fashioned synonym for *wonderful* — recognising it helps you read poetry, lyrics, and literary fiction.
